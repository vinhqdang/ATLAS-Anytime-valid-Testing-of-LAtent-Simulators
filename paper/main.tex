\title{\textbf{ATLAS: Anytime-valid Testing of Latent Simulators}\\[2pt]
\large Sequential certification of world-model faithfulness via e-process betting}
\author{Vinh Dang}
\date{}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
A world model (latent simulator) is useful only if its imagined rollouts stay
faithful to reality---but faithfulness is not static. It \emph{decays with the
rollout horizon} through compounding error, and it \emph{drifts over deployment
time} through non-stationarity, the sim-to-real gap, and the self-referential
feedback loop of model-based reinforcement learning, in which an improving policy
steers the agent into states the model never trained on. Current practice evaluates
world models with one-off offline metrics (FVD, PSNR, return gaps) that are blind to
this online degradation. We introduce \textbf{ATLAS}, which equips a deployed world
model with a \emph{running, statistically valid} certificate: as of round $t$, the
model's rollouts are trustworthy up to horizon $\hstar(t)$ at tolerance $\eps$, with
a guarantee that holds \emph{uniformly over all time}. ATLAS scores each horizon with
a proper scoring rule (making it representation-agnostic), bets against the null that
the horizon is $\eps$-faithful using a test martingale, and tracks the
\emph{faithfulness frontier} $\hstar(t)$---the largest horizon whose e-process has
not rejected. The construction is \emph{conditional} (one bet at a time), so it needs
no i.i.d.\ or exchangeability assumption and remains valid on the endogenous,
policy-dependent data stream that breaks conformal methods. We prove three results:
(i) a Ville-type guarantee that a truly faithful horizon is never revoked except with
probability $\le\alpha$; (ii) an \emph{anytime-valid simulation lemma} that turns the
running certified one-step error into a time-uniform bound on the $h$-step value gap;
and (iii) an order-optimal detection-delay bound under a drift alternative. We
validate all three theorems on a controllable latent simulator, then demonstrate
ATLAS on real image sequences (Moving MNIST and KTH Actions) under three distinct
distribution shifts, and on \emph{learned neural world models} (a convolutional
autoencoder and a JEPA-style frozen-encoder model) run on GPU. Across settings
$\hstar(t)$ collapses precisely when the shift opens, while offline metrics stay
blind.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

World models---action-conditioned generative or predictive models of an
environment's dynamics---are central to modern sequential decision making, from
Dreamer-style model-based agents to JEPA-style latent predictors and large video
simulators \citep{ha2018worldmodels,hafner2023dreamerv3,assran2023ijepa,bardes2024vjepa}.
A world model earns its keep by letting an agent \emph{imagine} rollouts and plan
against them. That value is entirely contingent on \emph{faithfulness}: the imagined
$h$-step future must match what reality would produce.

Faithfulness is neither uniform nor static. It \textbf{decays with horizon} because
recursive rollouts compound one-step error, so a model faithful at $h{=}1$ can be
useless at $h{=}20$; and it \textbf{drifts over deployment time} through
non-stationarity, the sim-to-real gap, and---most insidiously---the self-referential
feedback loop of model-based RL, where an improving policy pushes the agent into
regions the model never saw. Yet world models are still evaluated with one-off,
offline metrics: Fr\'echet Video Distance \citep{unterthiner2018fvd}, PSNR/SSIM,
return gaps. These are computed once, before or independent of deployment, and are
therefore \emph{blind by construction} to online degradation. There is no method that
gives a deployed world model a running certificate of the form
\begin{quote}\itshape
as of now, this model's rollouts are trustworthy up to horizon $\hstar(t)$ at error
level $\eps$,
\end{quote}
with a guarantee that holds uniformly over all time. That object is exactly what an
\emph{e-process} delivers and offline metrics cannot.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}\itemsep2pt
\item \textbf{A conditional e-process construction for world-model faithfulness on
endogenous streams} (\S\ref{sec:setup}--\ref{sec:method}). The data-generating policy
is a function of the model under test, so the stream is neither i.i.d.\ nor
exchangeable. ATLAS never invokes exchangeability---only a conditional-mean null under
the deployment filtration---which is what keeps it sound where conformal prediction
degrades.
\item \textbf{The faithfulness frontier $\hstar(t)$} (\S\ref{sec:method}): a
horizon-indexed family of dependent e-processes and a time-evolving trust-horizon
estimator. Because it is anytime-valid, a planner may legally clip its imagination
depth to $\hstar(t)$---turning the certificate into a controller.
\item \textbf{Three theorems} (\S\ref{sec:theory}): uniform validity (no false
revocation), an \emph{anytime-valid simulation lemma} linking certified one-step error
to the $h$-step value gap, and an order-optimal detection-delay bound.
\item \textbf{Empirical validation} (\S\ref{sec:experiments}): a ground-truth
simulation study confirming all three theorems; real-data experiments on Moving MNIST
and KTH Actions under speed, gait, and domain shifts; and learned neural world models
(conv-autoencoder and JEPA-style) run on GPU. In every case $\hstar(t)$ collapses when
the shift opens while an offline metric stays blind.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related work}\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Anytime-valid inference and testing by betting.}
ATLAS builds on Ville's inequality for nonnegative supermartingales
\citep{ville1939} and the theory of test martingales, e-values and e-processes
\citep{shafer2019game,vovk2021evalues,ramdas2023savi}. Our per-horizon test is a
testing-by-betting procedure \citep{shafer2021testing} using safe/GRO betting
\citep{gruenwald2024safe} and betting confidence sequences for bounded means
\citep{waudbysmith2024betting,howard2021timeuniform}. This machinery has not
previously been instantiated for \emph{world-model faithfulness} on
action-conditioned, policy-endogenous streams, nor as a horizon-indexed frontier.

\paragraph{Conformal prediction for time series and multi-step forecasting.}
Conformal prediction \citep{vovk2005algorithmic} and its distribution-shift variants
\citep{tibshirani2019covariate,gibbs2021adaptive,gibbs2024conformal} target
(approximately) exchangeable or bounded-drift single-step prediction and give marginal
coverage, not an anytime-valid per-horizon certificate, and they do not accommodate
policy-endogenous data. ATLAS's conditional construction sidesteps exchangeability
entirely.

\paragraph{World-model evaluation.}
Metrics such as FVD \citep{unterthiner2018fvd} and reconstruction/return gaps are
offline and static. ATLAS supplies the online, guaranteed trust horizon this
literature lacks.

\paragraph{Model-based RL and the simulation lemma.}
The classical simulation lemma bounds a value gap by average one-step model error
\citep{kearns2002near,janner2019mbpo,lobel2024tightness}, but at a fixed sample size
and assuming the one-step error is known. Our Theorem~\ref{thm:simlemma} is a
time-uniform, empirically certified version driven by an online confidence sequence.

\paragraph{Sequential change detection.}
For the drift alternative we use e-detectors \citep{shin2024edetectors}, whose
Shiryaev--Roberts form and Lorden/Lai-style delay theory
\citep{lorden1971,lai1998information} we specialize to a score-divergence
faithfulness null with a horizon frontier.

\smallskip
The nearest anytime-valid neighbours audit agent \emph{behaviour} or LLM-evaluation
drift; ATLAS instead audits what the agent's world model \emph{believes}, occupying
distinct ground.

\section{Problem setup}\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Deployment protocol.} A world model $M$ is deployed online with a
policy/planner $\pi$. At round $t$ the agent is in (latent) state $x_t$; the planner
selects an action sequence $a_{t:t+H-1}$ using $M$; for each horizon $h\in\{1,\dots,H\}$
the model emits an action-conditioned predictive distribution
$P^M_{t,h}(\cdot)=P^M(\cdot\mid x_t,a_{t:t+h-1})\in\Delta(\Ycal)$ over a target space
$\Ycal$ (observations, states, or a learned latent code). Reality later reveals the
realized outcome $Y^{(h)}_t\in\Ycal$, resolving the round-$t$, horizon-$h$ prediction
at time $t{+}h$. We make \emph{no} i.i.d.\ or exchangeability assumption.

\paragraph{Nonconformity scores.} Fix a negatively-oriented, strictly proper scoring
rule $S:\Delta(\Ycal)\times\Ycal\to[0,B]$ \citep{gneiting2007strictly}. Canonical
choices---energy score / CRPS for pixels, Gaussian log-score or a latent Mahalanobis
score for Dreamer/JEPA latents---make ATLAS \emph{representation-agnostic}: the latent
score compares a predicted embedding to the realized embedding under the model's own
metric, so decoding is unnecessary. For a strictly proper $S$ the score divergence
$\dS(Q,P)=\E_{Y\sim Q}[S(P,Y)]-\E_{Y\sim Q}[S(Q,Y)]\ge0$, with equality iff $P=Q$
(KL for the log score; an energy/MMD metric for the energy score; squared $W_2$ for a
Gaussian score).

\paragraph{$\eps$-faithfulness null.} For horizon $h$ and tolerance $\eps_h\ge0$,
\[
H_0^{(h)}:\quad \dS\!\big(Q_{s,h},P^M_{s,h}\big)\le \eps_h \ \text{ for every issue-time } s,
\]
where $Q_{s,h}=\mathrm{Law}(Y^{(h)}_s\mid\Fcal_s)$ is the true conditional law.
$\eps_h{=}0$ is exact conditional calibration.

\paragraph{The endogeneity problem.} The stream is policy-dependent: the distribution
of future states depends on actions the planner chose by imagining rollouts \emph{inside
$M$}. As $\pi$ improves it steers the agent into $M$'s worst regions---exactly where
we most need to test. The outcomes are neither i.i.d.\ nor exchangeable, so
split/full conformal and their time-series variants lose coverage. ATLAS instead uses
the deployment filtration $\Fcal_k$ (all information up to the $k$-th resolution) and
requires only \emph{conditional} validity, $\E[e_k\mid\Fcal_{k-1}]\le1$ under
$H_0^{(h)}$. Because this is a conditional statement it holds for \emph{any} predictable
policy and betting rule---the endogenous stream is handled for free.

\section{Method}\label{sec:method}

\paragraph{Per-horizon betting e-process.} Let $Z_k\in[-B,B]$ be a per-round score
statistic whose conditional mean is bounded by $\eps_h$ under $H_0^{(h)}$ (e.g.\ the
excess of the model's score over a predictable calibrated reference, or the
calibration excess $\mathrm{mahalanobis}^2/d-1$ of a Gaussian latent predictive, which
has mean $0$ under exact calibration). With a predictable bet
$\lambda_k\in[0,\lambda_{\max}]$, $\lambda_{\max}=1/(z_{\mathrm{hi}}-z_{\mathrm{lo}})$,
\[
e_k = 1+\lambda_k\,(Z_k-\eps_h),\qquad
\E[e_k\mid\Fcal_{k-1}]=1+\lambda_k\big(\E[Z_k\mid\Fcal_{k-1}]-\eps_h\big)\le 1,
\]
so the wealth $W^{(h)}_K=\prod_{k\le K}e_k$ is a test supermartingale
\citep{waudbysmith2024betting}. We use \emph{mixture betting}---the average of
$\prod_k(1+\lambda(Z_k-\eps_h))$ over a grid of $\lambda$---which is parameter-free,
does not deplete wealth under the null, and reacts quickly to an unknown post-change
margin (the GRO principle of \citealp{gruenwald2024safe}).

\paragraph{Multi-step validity.} Overlapping horizon-$h$ windows make consecutive
residuals a moving-average process with $\E[Z_k\mid\Fcal_{k-1}]\ne0$---the
recursive-multi-step failure of naive multi-step conformal prediction. ATLAS resolves
this by processing each horizon on \emph{non-overlapping} (stride-$h$) windows,
restoring conditional validity; the (real) cost is $h$-fold fewer updates at horizon
$h$, honestly reflecting that long horizons are harder to certify.

\paragraph{The faithfulness frontier.} Run one e-process per horizon and declare $h$
\emph{revoked} the first time $W^{(h)}\ge1/\alpha$ (a permanent, latching stopping
decision). The trust horizon is
\[
\hstar(t)=\max\{\,h: \text{all horizons} \le h \text{ not yet revoked}\,\},
\]
a monotone frontier matching ``clip imagination depth'': a broken short horizon
implies broken longer ones. For simultaneous validity over all faithful horizons we
run each at level $\alpha/H$ (union bound) or drive the frontier off an averaged
e-process (an average of e-values is an e-value \citep{vovk2021evalues}, valid under
arbitrary dependence across horizons). For the drift setting we use a
Shiryaev--Roberts \emph{e-detector} \citep{shin2024edetectors}.

\section{Theory}\label{sec:theory}

We use: \textbf{(A1)} bounded score $S\in[0,B]$; \textbf{(A2)} predictable bets and
reference (no i.i.d./exchangeability); \textbf{(A3)} a proper-score-to-metric modulus
$\rho(Q,P)\le\phi(\dS(Q,P))$ (e.g.\ Pinsker); \textbf{(A4)} rollout regularity
($(\gamma,R_{\max})$ discounting or $L$-Lipschitz dynamics).

\begin{theorem}[Uniform validity]\label{thm:validity}
Fix $h$ and $\alpha\in(0,1)$. Under $H_0^{(h)}$ and \emph{(A1)--(A2)},
$\Pr(\exists K: W^{(h)}_K\ge 1/\alpha)\le\alpha$. Hence the probability that ATLAS
\emph{ever} revokes a truly $\eps_h$-faithful horizon is at most $\alpha$, uniformly
over all rounds and all predictable policies and betting rules. Running each of $H$
horizons at level $\alpha/H$ controls the whole frontier simultaneously.
\end{theorem}
\noindent\emph{Proof sketch.} (A2) makes each $e_k$ a conditional e-variable and (A1)
plus the $\lambda_{\max}$ cap keep $e_k\ge0$, so $W^{(h)}$ is a nonnegative
supermartingale with $W_0=1$; Ville's inequality gives the bound. No exchangeability
enters. \hfill$\square$

\begin{theorem}[Anytime-valid simulation lemma]\label{thm:simlemma}
Instantiate the construction at $h{=}1$ with mixture betting. The set
$\mathrm{CS}_K=\{\eps: W^{(1)}_K(\eps)<1/\alpha\}$ is a $(1-\alpha)$ confidence sequence
for the running one-step gap $\delta_1(K)$, with variance-adaptive (empirical-Bernstein)
upper edge $\hat\delta_1(K)$. Under \emph{(A1)--(A4)}, with probability $\ge1-\alpha$,
\emph{for all rounds $t$ and horizons $h\le H$ simultaneously},
\[
\big|V^\pi_{M,h}(t)-V^\pi_{\mathrm{real},h}(t)\big|\ \le\ C(h,\gamma,L)\,\phi\!\big(\hat\delta_1(t)\big),
\quad
C(h,\gamma,L)=\frac{\gamma(1-\gamma^h)}{(1-\gamma)^2}R_{\max}\ \text{ or }\ R_{\max}\!\sum_{i=1}^{h}L^{i-1}.
\]
\end{theorem}
\noindent\emph{Proof sketch.} Theorem~\ref{thm:validity} read as a confidence sequence
gives the empirical-Bernstein $\hat\delta_1$; telescoping one-step error along the
rollout and converting the certified score gap to the metric $\rho$ via (A3) gives the
$h$-step bound. Every quantity on the right is observed and certified online. \hfill$\square$

\begin{theorem}[Detection delay]\label{thm:delay}
Suppose faithfulness breaks at an unknown changepoint $\tau$ with post-change margin
$\E[Z_k\mid\Fcal_{k-1}]\ge\eps_1+\Delta$ and variance $\le\sigma^2$. Run the ATLAS
e-detector with GRO betting and alarm at $1/\alpha$. Then false alarms are controlled
($\Pr(T<\infty\mid H_0)\le\alpha$ or $\E_\infty[T]\ge1/\alpha$), and with probability
$\ge1-\alpha$ the delay obeys $T-\tau\le \log(1/\alpha)/D^\star\,(1+o(1))$, where
$D^\star=\sup_\lambda\E_{\mathrm{post}}[\log(1+\lambda(Z-\eps_1))]$; for the bounded
score $D^\star\ge \Delta^2/(2(\sigma^2+B\Delta/3))$, matching the Lorden/Lai lower
bound up to constants.
\end{theorem}
\noindent\emph{Proof sketch.} Each start-time product is a test supermartingale (false
alarm); after $\tau$ the summand started at $\tau{+}1$ grows at rate $D^\star$, so
crossing $\log(1/\alpha)$ takes $\approx\log(1/\alpha)/D^\star$ rounds; the bound on
$D^\star$ is the second-order (Bernstein) expansion of $\log(1+x)$. \hfill$\square$

\section{Experiments}\label{sec:experiments}

Code, all figures, and reproduction scripts accompany the paper. We report three
tiers: a ground-truth simulation study (\S\ref{sec:exp-synth}), real image-sequence
data (\S\ref{sec:exp-real}), and learned neural world models on GPU

\subsection{Ground-truth validation of Theorems~1--3}\label{sec:exp-synth}

We use a controllable linear-Gaussian latent simulator---the one setting where the
true dynamics are known, so the guarantees can be checked against ground truth. The
world model is exact pre-change; an unmodelled force is injected mid-deployment. A
behaviour policy draws exogenous exploration actions, making the action-conditioned
predictive exactly calibrated under the null (a genuine null to test) while the state
stream remains non-i.i.d.\ and adaptive.

\paragraph{Theorem~1 (validity).} Under the null (no drift), across $400$ independent
deployments, the empirical false-revocation rate is at or below the level, uniformly
over all rounds (Table~\ref{tab:validity}, Fig.~\ref{fig:validity}). The e-process is
conservative, as Ville's inequality guarantees.

\paragraph{Theorem~2 (frontier collapse \& simulation lemma).} With a drift injected
at round $900$, the certified one-step gap $\hat\delta_1(t)$ stays low while faithful
and rises exactly when the drift opens; the trust horizon
$\hstar(t)=\max\{h:C(h,\gamma)\phi(\hat\delta_1(t))\le\text{budget}\}$ collapses from
$38$ to $7$ within a few rounds, while an offline metric frozen at its pre-deployment
value stays at $38$---blind and dangerously overconfident (Fig.~\ref{fig:frontier}).

\paragraph{Theorem~3 (detection delay).} Sweeping the drift margin and measuring
detection delay (mixture e-process, $100$ seeds) confirms the law
$\text{delay}\approx(\log(1/\alpha)+\log m)/D^\star$: the measured delay matches the
prediction to $\approx6\%$, with $\text{delay}\cdot D^\star\approx6$ nearly constant
(Fig.~\ref{fig:delay}). Delay is $\propto1/D^\star$; here $D^\star\propto m$ because the
bet is capped at $\lambda_{\max}$ (the $\propto m^2$ small-margin regime requires
$m\lesssim0.07$).

\subsection{Real-world data}\label{sec:exp-real}

We monitor a learned latent world model (PCA features + a stabilized linear latent
dynamics model---a lightweight but genuine latent simulator) on real image sequences
under three distinct shifts. The tolerance $\eps_h$ is calibrated on held-out
in-distribution clips (on the same clipped statistic the e-process consumes, since
real-video excess is heavy-tailed); deployment runs in-distribution and then switches
regime.

\begin{itemize}\itemsep2pt
\item \textbf{Moving MNIST \citep{srivastava2015movingmnist}, speed shift} (native
$\to$ $2\times$). Wealth is flat during the native period (valid, no depletion),
$\hstar{=}8$; after the shift, horizons $h{=}1,2,3$ cross the threshold and $\hstar$
collapses to $0$ while the offline horizon stays at $8$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:real}, left).
\item \textbf{KTH Actions \citep{schuldt2004kth}, gait dynamics shift}
(walking $\to$ running). A walking-trained model is monitored; $\hstar{=}3$ throughout
walking (no false revocation), then collapses to $0$ once running begins
(Fig.~\ref{fig:real}, middle).
\item \textbf{KTH Actions, domain shift} (outdoor $\to$ indoor, same action). A subtler
shift: $\hstar{=}3$ outdoors, then a \emph{partial} collapse to $2$ indoors (only the
longest horizon is revoked). The graded response---full collapse for strong dynamics
shifts, partial for a subtle domain shift---is itself informative
(Fig.~\ref{fig:real}, right).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Learned neural world models on GPU}\label{sec:exp-gpu}

We replace the linear WM with \emph{learned neural} world models behind the identical
\texttt{encode/predict/excess\_stream} interface, run on an NVIDIA T4 GPU.

\begin{itemize}\itemsep2pt
\item \textbf{Conv-autoencoder WM on Moving MNIST} (a Dreamer-like latent model).
Wealth is flat during the native period; $\hstar$ collapses $3\to0$ after the $2\times$
speed shift (Fig.~\ref{fig:gpu}, left). ATLAS's guarantees hold behind a genuinely
learned model.
\item \textbf{JEPA-style WM on KTH walking$\to$running.} A frozen ImageNet ResNet-18

in-distribution walking excess is low and stable ($\approx0.1$ at $h{=}1$) while running
is clearly separated ($\approx1.1$). $\hstar{=}3$ holds through the entire walking
period (valid null) and collapses to $0$ once running begins (Fig.~\ref{fig:gpu},

clean on real video; a full DreamerV3 RSSM drops in behind the same interface.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion and limitations}

ATLAS's validity rests solely on the conditional-mean null under the deployment
filtration; it makes no i.i.d./exchangeability assumption, which is what makes it sound
on endogenous, policy-dependent data. Three honest caveats. (i) The controlled
simulation study uses an exogenous behaviour policy to obtain an exact null; the fully
policy-in-the-loop regime is future work, though the conditional construction is valid
there for the same reason. (ii) Non-overlapping windows make long horizons genuinely
slower to certify (fewer effective samples). (iii) Certificate quality is bounded by
world-model quality: a low-capacity or overfit model yields a non-stationary
in-distribution null; a representation that generalizes (e.g.\ a frozen pretrained
encoder, or a stronger RSSM) restores a clean certificate, as our JEPA experiment
shows. Tolerances $\eps_h$ are modeling choices that define ``faithful''; we calibrate
them to the in-distribution level so ATLAS certifies \emph{departures}.

\section{Conclusion}

ATLAS gives a deployed world model a running, anytime-valid certificate of
faithfulness---a trust horizon $\hstar(t)$ that collapses precisely when reality drifts
from imagination, backed by a uniform no-false-revocation guarantee, an anytime-valid
simulation lemma, and an order-optimal detection-delay bound. It is representation- and
model-agnostic, needs no exchangeability, and turns the certificate into a planning
controller. Empirically it behaves as the theory predicts across synthetic, real-video,
and learned-neural-world-model settings, while offline metrics stay blind.
